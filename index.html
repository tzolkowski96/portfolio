01 About

My job title says data analyst, but the work is mostly engineering. I design ETL pipelines that pull clinical data from a dozen different systems. I build APIs that let researchers query datasets without knowing SQL. I automate the boring stuff so scientists can focus on science.

The path here wasn't obvious. I studied International Studies at UW-Madison, thinking I'd end up in policy or diplomacy. But I kept gravitating toward the data side of every project. Grad school for Information Science made it official. That international lens stuck though. I think about data as translation: taking information from one context and making it useful in another.

Outside work, I write. Data journalism on Medium, where I dig into public datasets and explain what's actually happening. Personal essays on Substack. And occasional pro bono consulting for organizations that need data help but can't afford it.

02 Work
IU School of Medicine 2023 — Present

Data Analyst, Medical and Molecular Genetics

Started on the Core team, promoted to Career track in 2026. The research team had data scattered across clinical systems, genetic databases, and legacy spreadsheets. Nothing talked to anything else. I built the connective tissue: ETL pipelines that normalize data from different sources, APIs that let researchers query without writing SQL, automated workflows that flag data quality issues before they become problems.

The hardest part wasn't technical. It was learning enough neurology to understand what the researchers actually needed.

Telkomsel 2022

Business Data Analyst Intern

Indonesia's largest mobile operator. 170 million subscribers. I worked on customer behavior analysis, trying to predict churn before it happened. Built dashboards that product teams actually used, which is rarer than it sounds.

First time working with data at genuine scale. Changed how I think about query optimization.

UW-Madison 2019 — 2021

IT Support → Data Manager

Started answering help desk tickets. Ended up managing research datasets for a sociology department that had decades of survey data in formats nobody remembered how to read. Wrote migration scripts, documented everything, learned that institutional data is always messier than you expect.
